Case Study: UPI Payment Fraud via Online Marketplaces
Rohan, a college student, listed his old laptop for ₹15,000 on a university Facebook group to help pay for his textbooks. Within an hour, a "buyer" contacted him, quickly agreeing to the full price without haggling. To build credibility, the buyer claimed to be a busy military officer and shared a forged army ID card.
Stating he couldn't meet in person or pay cash, the officer insisted on using Google Pay. However, instead of transferring the funds, the scammer sent a custom QR code to Rohan's phone. Over a hurried phone call, he aggressively pressured the student: "Just scan this code and enter your UPI PIN to receive the funds instantly."
Unaware that a UPI PIN is strictly required for sending money—never for receiving it—Rohan complied. Instantly, the trap was sprung. Instead of receiving payment, ₹15,000 was authorized for deduction from Rohan’s account and transferred directly to the scammer.
The consequences of this Unified Payments Interface (UPI) fraud were devastating. Beyond the severe immediate financial loss, which heavily impacted Rohan's tight student budget, he endured immense emotional stress. His trust in digital marketplaces like Facebook and OLX evaporated entirely. Furthermore, Rohan was left to navigate the exhausting, time-consuming hassle of freezing his bank accounts and filing a cybercrime police report.
This scenario highlights a rampant financial crime where scammers exploit digital platforms, manipulating vulnerable individuals into authorizing their own financial theft under the guise of receiving a payment.
